# 10 Common English Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Even advanced English learners make common mistakes. Understanding these errors and knowing how to correct them can significantly improve your English skills.

## 1. Confusing "Their," "They're," and "There"

**Wrong:** Their going to the store.
**Right:** They're going to the store.

- **Their** = possessive (their book)
- **They're** = they are
- **There** = location or existence

## 2. Using "Less" vs. "Fewer"

**Wrong:** There are less people here today.
**Right:** There are fewer people here today.

- **Fewer** = countable items (fewer books)
- **Less** = uncountable items (less water)

## 3. Subject-Verb Agreement

**Wrong:** The team are winning.
**Right:** The team is winning.

Collective nouns (team, family, group) are usually treated as singular in American English.

## 4. Misusing "It's" and "Its"

**Wrong:** The dog wagged it's tail.
**Right:** The dog wagged its tail.

- **It's** = it is or it has
- **Its** = possessive

## 5. Incorrect Preposition Usage

**Wrong:** I'm good in English.
**Right:** I'm good at English.

Prepositions can be tricky. Common patterns:
- Good **at** (skills)
- Interested **in** (topics)
- Depend **on** (reliance)

## 6. Double Negatives

**Wrong:** I don't have nothing.
**Right:** I don't have anything.

In standard English, avoid using two negative words in the same clause.

## 7. Confusing "Affect" and "Effect"

**Wrong:** The weather effected our plans.
**Right:** The weather affected our plans.

- **Affect** = verb (to influence)
- **Effect** = noun (a result)

## 8. Using "Could of" Instead of "Could have"

**Wrong:** I could of done better.
**Right:** I could have done better.

"Could of" is never correct. The contraction "could've" sounds like "could of," causing this common mistake.

## 9. Misplacing Apostrophes

**Wrong:** The student's are studying.
**Right:** The students are studying.

Apostrophes show possession or contraction, not plurals.

## 10. Using "Literally" Incorrectly

**Wrong:** I literally died laughing.
**Right:** I was laughing so hard.

"Literally" means actually, in reality. Unless something actually happened, don't use "literally."
